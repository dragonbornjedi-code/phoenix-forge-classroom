# Curriculum Of Life

Teacher Edition source of truth for Ezra's five-year-old homeschool framework.

The seven sections are adult-facing planning categories. Student Edition should translate them into short games, missions, visuals, rewards, and simple choices.

## Design Rules
- Keep lessons concrete, playful, and brief.
- Use the technical language as the teacher framework; use kid language in Student Edition.
- Prefer physical interaction before screen interaction.
- Track observable behavior, not labels or diagnoses.
- Offer several teaching methods because one method will not work for every child or every day.
- Separate support from punishment. Emotional regulation tools are not consequences.
- Treat progress as version control: observe, adjust, retry.

## 1. Cognitive & Academic Foundations

Teacher framing: literacy, numeracy, scientific thinking, first-principles reasoning, history as systems, and abstraction mapping.

Student framing: secret-code reading, guess-and-check science, spot-the-error games, then-and-now stories, and "what job does this thing do?"

Lesson patterns:
- Label Hunt: label house objects, components, tools, and containers. Ask him to find, match, or explain one label.
- Guess And Check: make a prediction, run a tiny test, and record what happened with a sticker, drawing, or one sentence.
- Manual Mission: follow a picture-based manual for Lego, a toy, or a simple build.
- Then And Now: compare an old tool, toy, or machine to a modern version.
- System Map: draw where water, power, food, toys, or information goes.

Progress metrics:
- Recognizes and uses new symbols or labels.
- Makes a prediction before testing.
- Changes an answer when evidence changes.
- Follows 2-4 visual steps with less help over time.
- Explains an object by job, parts, or category.

Teacher cues:
- If he guesses without looking, slow down and ask, "What do your eyes notice?"
- If he gets stuck, reduce the step size.
- If he memorizes but cannot transfer, try a new object with the same pattern.
- If frustration rises, switch to a physical version of the same idea.

Support methods:
- Visual: picture cards, labels, maps, diagrams.
- Physical: sorting, building, tracing, pointing, moving pieces.
- Verbal: "I wonder..." prompts and simple why chains.
- Choice-based: offer two possible answers and let him test.

## 2. Emotional Intelligence & Self-Regulation

Teacher framing: emotional literacy, state awareness, protocol execution, self-regulation, agency, and mindfulness.

Student framing: system temperature, pause button, deep breath trick, safe fort, "I did this," and body check.

Lesson patterns:
- System Temp: blue means calm, yellow means heating up, red means too much.
- Pause Button: freeze, count, breathe, then choose.
- Recovery Hub: a calm place for reset, not isolation or punishment.
- Energy Meter: show 1-5 fingers for energy, hunger, tiredness, or need for help.
- Success Jar: mark moments where he tried, repaired, asked, or recovered.

Progress metrics:
- Names or points to a feeling color.
- Uses a pause cue with adult help.
- Returns from upset faster over time.
- Asks for help, food, rest, or space.
- Can say what helped after a hard moment.

Teacher cues:
- Fast movement, louder voice, clenched hands, refusal, or silly escalation may mean yellow state.
- Red state is not the teaching window. Regulate first, discuss later.
- Repeated refusal may mean task size, sensory load, fatigue, hunger, or unclear instructions.
- If one breathing tool fails, switch methods instead of insisting.

Support methods:
- Co-regulation: calm voice, fewer words, grounded posture.
- Movement: wall push, carry a weighted item, shake-out, walk.
- Breath: feather blow, candle breath, box breathing only if tolerated.
- Sensory: dimmer light, headphones, blanket, water, quiet.
- Repair: short post-moment review with "what happened, what helped, what next?"

## 3. Practical Life Skills

Teacher framing: body maintenance, financial literacy, household competence, tool safety, and digital citizenship.

Student framing: charge the battery, fuel sort, token shop, room bug hunt, fix-it station, and private-stuff rules.

Lesson patterns:
- Body Battery: connect sleep, food, water, hygiene, and movement to energy.
- Token Ledger: earn, save, spend, and pitch a reason for a reward or part.
- System Audit: use a flashlight or checklist to find 3-5 out-of-place items.
- Tool Drill: practice safe carrying, asking, using, and returning.
- Trusted Team: identify who can know private information or help online.

Progress metrics:
- Completes one routine with fewer prompts.
- Saves tokens for a delayed reward.
- Gives a simple reason for a purchase or choice.
- Uses tools with safe handling reminders.
- Knows at least one private-information rule.

Teacher cues:
- If routines collapse, check time of day, hunger, fatigue, and transition load.
- If tokens become stressful, simplify to fewer categories.
- If cleanup is overwhelming, reduce to "find three things."
- If privacy lessons feel abstract, use roleplay with toys.

Support methods:
- Visual checklist.
- First-then board.
- Timer or song.
- Token board.
- Model-copy-practice.

## 4. Interpersonal & Social Dynamics

Teacher framing: empathy, perspective-taking, conflict resolution, boundaries, access control, and collaboration.

Student framing: puppet feelings, talk-it-out, turn timer, my bubble, ask first, and helper missions.

Lesson patterns:
- Puppet POV: ask what the puppet wants, feels, or needs.
- Repeat Back: he repeats one thing he heard before responding.
- Turn Timer: use visible time for shared toys and games.
- Bubble Map: draw personal space and practice stop/ask/wait.
- Team Build: assign simple roles like holder, builder, checker.

Progress metrics:
- Identifies another person's likely feeling or want.
- Uses a boundary phrase or stop signal.
- Waits for a turn with support.
- Repairs after conflict with a short action.
- Completes a shared task with a role.

Teacher cues:
- Grabbing may mean impulse, unclear turn structure, or fear of losing access.
- Boundary refusal should be respected even when phrased imperfectly.
- If conflict language fails, switch to timer, visual turn card, or adult narration.

Support methods:
- Social story.
- Puppet rehearsal.
- Visual turn cards.
- Timer.
- Role swap.

## 5. Physical Mastery & Environmental Guidance

Teacher framing: body control, proprioception, manual dexterity, resource loops, survival, and situational awareness.

Student framing: balance machine, tiny-hands missions, trash sorting, light patrol, house map, and safe spot.

Lesson patterns:
- Tape Line: balance, hop, crawl, or carry across a line.
- Micro Build: beads, nuts and bolts, clay, blocks, tweezers, or safe jumper wires.
- Resource Sort: compost, recycle, trash, keep, repair.
- Power Patrol: find lights or devices to turn off.
- Home Map: draw doors, safe spots, family meeting points.

Progress metrics:
- Holds balance longer or with better control.
- Completes fine-motor task with less dropping.
- Sorts common materials correctly.
- Identifies home exits and safe places.
- Uses body control near fragile tools or builds.

Teacher cues:
- Clumsiness may increase with fatigue, excitement, hunger, or overstimulation.
- Fine motor work should stop before hands become frustrated.
- Survival lessons should feel secure, not scary.

Support methods:
- Big movement before fine motor.
- Short repetitions.
- Heavier objects for proprioceptive input.
- Demonstrate slowly.
- Use real household jobs.

## 6. Creative Expression & Intellectual Curiosity

Teacher framing: procedural art, maker pipeline, philosophical inquiry, curiosity, and failure recovery.

Student framing: pattern art, draw-plan-build-upgrade, great why, what-if stories, and oops patch.

Lesson patterns:
- Rule Art: create with one rule, like only circles or red-blue-red-blue.
- Maker Pipeline: draw, mock up, build, upgrade.
- Why Chain: ask why three times, then look up or test one answer.
- What If: imagine unusual worlds and consequences.
- Oops Patch: name the mistake, make one change, retry.

Progress metrics:
- Creates a pattern or follows a creative constraint.
- Moves from idea to prototype.
- Suggests one improvement.
- Uses "oops" without quitting.
- Asks a question and pursues an answer.

Teacher cues:
- Perfectionism may show up as avoidance.
- Too many materials can block creativity; constrain choices.
- Curiosity often appears as mess, repetition, and odd questions.

Support methods:
- Limited material sets.
- Before-and-after photos.
- One-upgrade rule.
- Adult scribing for ideas.
- Celebrate retries more than outcomes.

## 7. Values, Ethics & Civic Duty

Teacher framing: moral reasoning, integrity, decision architecture, community contribution, calibration, and version control.

Student framing: fairness games, daily promise, truth box, two paths, helper mission, and version check.

Lesson patterns:
- Fair Split: divide snacks, turns, or materials.
- Daily Promise: choose one tiny promise and check it later.
- Two Paths: choose between two actions and discuss what happens next.
- Helper Node: do one useful act for the home or another person.
- Version Check: compare yesterday and today without shame.

Progress metrics:
- Keeps a small promise or repairs when he cannot.
- Identifies fair/unfair in concrete examples.
- Predicts one consequence of a choice.
- Helps with a family task.
- Names one thing he improved.

Teacher cues:
- Shame blocks learning. Use repair and retry.
- Abstract morality needs concrete examples.
- Praise specific actions, not fixed identity.
- If he lies, investigate fear, impulse, or wishful thinking before consequences.

Support methods:
- Story examples.
- Toy roleplay.
- Concrete fairness objects.
- Repair script.
- Star or version log.

## Weekly Teacher Audit

Use this as the Teacher Edition weekly review model.

For each section:
- One win observed.
- One friction point.
- One method that worked.
- One method to try next.
- One metric to track next week.

Overall:
- Best energy window.
- Hardest transition.
- Strongest motivator.
- Current overload signs.
- Next tiny V1.1 adjustment.

## Student Edition Boundary

Student Edition should show:
- Missions.
- Games.
- Pictures.
- Simple rewards.
- Short choices.
- Friendly progress.

Student Edition should not show:
- Adult behavioral analysis.
- Diagnostic language.
- Long curriculum theory.
- Private teacher notes.
- Parent strategy prompts.
