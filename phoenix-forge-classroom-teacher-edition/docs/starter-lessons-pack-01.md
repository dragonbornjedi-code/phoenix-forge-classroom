# Starter Lessons Pack 01

Seven detailed lessons, one for each top-tier category. These are real Teacher Edition lesson objects that can also become Student Edition missions.

Default learner stage: five and a half.

## Lesson 1: Secret Label Decoder

Category: Cognitive & Academic Foundations.

Subcategories:
- Early Literacy.
- Documentation Literacy.
- Abstraction Mapping.

Primary methods:
- Explicit teaching.
- Play-based learning.
- Retrieval practice.
- UDL visual supports.

Student Edition mission:
Find the secret labels around the room and match them to the right objects.

Duration:
- Standard: 10-15 minutes.
- Low-demand: 3 labels in 3 minutes.

Materials:
- Sticky notes or index cards.
- Marker.
- 5-10 household objects.
- Optional picture cards.
- Finished box.

Objective:
Ezra will match printed labels to real objects and explain one object by its job.

Why it matters:
This connects reading to useful information. Words become tools for finding, organizing, and solving.

Setup:
Write simple labels: cup, door, bed, light, book, tool, sock, box. Add one picture if needed.

Steps:
1. Show one label and say, "This word is a clue. It says cup."
2. Model matching the label to the cup.
3. Ask him to place the next label.
4. When he places it, ask, "What job does this thing do?"
5. Put completed labels in the finished box or leave them attached.
6. End with retrieval: point to 3 labels and ask, "Which one says book?"

Questions:
- What do your eyes notice?
- What sound does this word start with?
- What job does this object do?
- Is this object also part of a bigger system?

Metric:
- Number of labels matched.
- Prompt level: model, verbal prompt, gesture, independent.
- Whether he explains object job.

Evidence:
- Photo of labeled objects.
- Teacher note on which labels were independent.

Supports:
- Visual: picture label.
- Sensory: stand and move label to object.
- Communication: point instead of saying.
- Transition: "First 3 labels, then build."

Make easier:
- Use 2 labels and obvious objects.
- Use pictures beside words.
- Let him choose between two objects.

Make harder:
- Remove pictures.
- Add beginning sound sorting.
- Ask him to create a new label.

Recovery:
If frustration rises, switch to "adult labels wrong object and he fixes it."

Student Edition notes:
Show 3 mission cards: Find Cup, Find Book, Find Light. Reward: Decoder Badge.

## Lesson 2: Build The System Temp Gauge

Category: Emotional Intelligence & Self-Regulation.

Subcategories:
- Feeling Identification.
- Energy And Arousal.
- Independent Regulation.
- Safety During Big Feelings.

Primary methods:
- Visual supports.
- Co-regulation.
- Explicit teaching.
- Roleplay.

Student Edition mission:
Build your color gauge: blue, yellow, red.

Duration:
- Standard: 15 minutes.
- Low-demand: point to one color and take one breath.

Materials:
- Paper plate or paper.
- Blue, yellow, red crayons/markers.
- Clothespin, arrow, or sticker marker.
- Optional emotion face cards.

Objective:
Ezra will identify a body/emotion state using blue/yellow/red and practice one matching support.

Why it matters:
The color system creates shared language before emotions get too big for words.

Setup:
Draw three zones: blue calm, yellow heating, red too much. Keep wording simple.

Steps:
1. Explain: "This is not good or bad. It tells us what your system needs."
2. Model yourself: "I am blue because my body feels calm."
3. Roleplay yellow: tense hands, frustrated face. Move marker to yellow.
4. Practice support: pause, wall push, feather breath, or water break.
5. Roleplay red with a puppet. Puppet goes to Recovery Hub.
6. Ask him to point to how he feels now.

Questions:
- What color is your engine?
- What does yellow need?
- Where can red go to get safe?
- What helped your body?

Metric:
- Identifies color with point or word.
- Accepts one support.
- Can match one support to one color.

Evidence:
- Photo of gauge.
- Teacher note: preferred support.

Supports:
- Visual: color gauge.
- Sensory: movement or pressure.
- Communication: point instead of explain.
- Transition: use gauge before hard tasks.

Make easier:
- Only blue and yellow today.
- Adult models; child only points.

Make harder:
- Add body signals: hands, heart, face, voice.
- Add "what helped?" after a real moment.

Recovery:
If he enters red, stop lesson. Use the gauge later when calm.

Student Edition notes:
Daily check-in can show three big buttons: Blue, Yellow, Red. Never show adult interpretation.

## Lesson 3: Snack Shop Token Pitch

Category: Practical Life Skills.

Subcategories:
- Financial Literacy.
- Nutrition And Hydration.
- Decision Architecture.
- Oral Language.

Primary methods:
- Concrete-representational-abstract.
- Play-based learning.
- Explicit teaching.
- Retrieval practice.

Student Edition mission:
Open the snack shop. Choose, count, and explain your snack.

Duration:
- Standard: 15-20 minutes.
- Low-demand: choose one snack and count one token.

Materials:
- 3 snack choices.
- 5 tokens.
- Paper "shop" signs.
- Cup of water.
- Optional small ledger.

Objective:
Ezra will use tokens to make a choice and give one reason for the choice.

Why it matters:
This connects money sense, food planning, agency, and simple reasoning.

Setup:
Assign simple token costs: apple slices 1, crackers 1, cheese 2, special treat 3.

Steps:
1. Show tokens and say, "Tokens help us choose."
2. Model buying water for 0 tokens because the body needs it.
3. Let him choose a snack.
4. Count tokens together.
5. Ask for a pitch: "Why does your body or brain need this?"
6. Record choice with a mark or sticker.
7. End with recall: "What did you buy? How many tokens?"

Questions:
- How many tokens does it cost?
- What does this food do for your body?
- Do you want to spend or save?
- What happens if you save one token?

Metric:
- Counts tokens with one-to-one correspondence.
- Gives one reason.
- Accepts spend/save choice.

Evidence:
- Ledger mark.
- Teacher note on counting accuracy and reason.

Supports:
- Visual: prices with dots.
- Sensory: crunchy/soft options.
- Communication: choose by pointing.
- Transition: snack shop after lesson block.

Make easier:
- Two choices, same cost.
- Adult counts aloud.

Make harder:
- Add save-for-later.
- Add "you have 3 tokens; what can you buy?"

Recovery:
If choice overload appears, reduce to two options and remove treat category.

Student Edition notes:
Child sees snack cards and token count. Teacher Edition keeps nutrition notes.

## Lesson 4: Puppet Peace Protocol

Category: Interpersonal & Social Dynamics.

Subcategories:
- Empathy And Perspective.
- Conflict Resolution.
- Boundaries And Consent.
- Listening And Reflecting.

Primary methods:
- Social narrative.
- Roleplay.
- Co-regulation.
- Explicit teaching.

Student Edition mission:
Help two puppets solve a toy problem.

Duration:
- Standard: 10-15 minutes.
- Low-demand: practice one phrase: "Can I have a turn?"

Materials:
- Two toys or puppets.
- One desirable object.
- Timer.
- Stop-sign card.

Objective:
Ezra will practice one conflict script: stop, listen, ask, solve.

Why it matters:
Conflict skills need rehearsal before real conflict. Puppets make it safer and less personal.

Setup:
Two puppets both want the same block/tool.

Steps:
1. Puppet A grabs. Puppet B says, "Stop."
2. Ask, "What does Puppet B want?"
3. Model repeat-back: "You wanted the block."
4. Offer two solutions: timer turn or trade.
5. Let Ezra choose a solution.
6. Practice the phrase with his voice or puppet voice.
7. Run the scene again with him as helper.

Questions:
- What does each puppet want?
- What can they say?
- What solution helps both?
- How do we repair if grabbing happened?

Metric:
- Identifies want/feeling.
- Uses or points to a boundary phrase.
- Chooses a solution.

Evidence:
- Teacher note on phrase used.
- Optional photo of peace mat/timer.

Supports:
- Visual: stop card.
- Sensory: peace mat.
- Communication: puppet speaks for him.
- Transition: use after sharing practice.

Make easier:
- Adult narrates; child chooses solution card.

Make harder:
- Add apology/repair.
- Add role swap.

Recovery:
If it becomes too real or frustrating, switch back to adult-only puppet show.

Student Edition notes:
Show puppet faces and solution buttons: Timer, Trade, Ask First.

## Lesson 5: Field Kit And Safe Map

Category: Physical Mastery & Environmental Guidance.

Subcategories:
- Situational Awareness.
- Field Survival Basics.
- Travel Readiness.
- Gross Motor.

Primary methods:
- Task analysis.
- Visual supports.
- Explicit teaching.
- Play-based movement.

Student Edition mission:
Pack your field kit and find the safe spots.

Duration:
- Standard: 20 minutes.
- Low-demand: pack 3 items.

Materials:
- Small backpack.
- Water bottle.
- Snack.
- Hat/jacket as weather requires.
- Simple map paper.
- Crayons.

Objective:
Ezra will pack a basic outing bag and identify one safe adult/safe spot.

Why it matters:
Travel readiness builds autonomy, safety, planning, and confidence.

Setup:
Choose a real or pretend destination: yard, park, library, car walk.

Steps:
1. Show destination: "We are going to the yard/park/library."
2. Ask, "What does our body need?"
3. Pack water, snack, clothing item, and comfort/safety item.
4. Draw a simple map: start, path, destination, safe spot.
5. Walk the path or pretend it with toys.
6. Practice rule: "If lost, stop and call for my adult."

Questions:
- What do we need if we get thirsty?
- Where is the safe spot?
- Who is your safe adult?
- What do you do if you cannot see me?

Metric:
- Packs items with prompt level.
- Identifies safe spot/adult.
- Follows route with support.

Evidence:
- Photo of packed kit or map.
- Teacher safety note.

Supports:
- Visual: packing checklist.
- Sensory: include comfort item.
- Communication: point to safe spot.
- Transition: preview before leaving.

Make easier:
- Pretend trip inside.
- Use 3-item checklist.

Make harder:
- Let him check weather and choose jacket/hat.
- Add map landmarks.

Recovery:
If safety talk creates anxiety, return to "helper packing mission" and keep tone calm.

Student Edition notes:
Show checklist icons: Water, Snack, Hat, Adult.

## Lesson 6: Cardboard Prototype Lab

Category: Creative Expression & Intellectual Curiosity.

Subcategories:
- Maker Pipeline.
- Design Thinking.
- Failure Recovery.
- Presentation And Sharing.

Primary methods:
- Project-based learning.
- Productive struggle.
- Task analysis.
- Play-based learning.

Student Edition mission:
Build V1, test it, then add one upgrade.

Duration:
- Standard: 25-35 minutes.
- Low-demand: build with blocks for 5 minutes and add one upgrade.

Materials:
- Cardboard scraps.
- Tape.
- Child-safe scissors with supervision if appropriate.
- Crayons/markers.
- Toy user: figure, car, stuffed toy.
- Camera for V1/V2 evidence.

Objective:
Ezra will create a prototype, test it with a toy user, and add one improvement.

Why it matters:
This builds the maker pipeline: idea, prototype, test, upgrade, explain.

Setup:
Prompt: "Build something that helps this toy cross, carry, sit, hide, or travel."

Steps:
1. Choose the toy user.
2. Ask, "What does the toy need?"
3. Draw a fast plan or point to materials.
4. Build V1.
5. Test with the toy.
6. Name one problem or one cool thing.
7. Add one upgrade.
8. Photograph V1 and V2.
9. Present: "I built __. I changed __."

Questions:
- Who is this for?
- What job does it do?
- What did V1 do well?
- What is one upgrade?

Metric:
- Moves from idea to build.
- Tests with toy.
- Adds one change.
- Explains change with word/point.

Evidence:
- V1/V2 photo.
- Teacher note on persistence and support level.

Supports:
- Visual: draw first.
- Sensory: offer tape instead of glue/mess.
- Communication: point to upgrade.
- Transition: timer before cleanup.

Make easier:
- Use blocks instead of cardboard.
- Adult tapes pieces while he places them.

Make harder:
- Add a constraint: must hold 3 blocks, must fit in a box, must use only 5 pieces.

Recovery:
If collapse causes frustration, say "Great test. The test found the patch." Reduce challenge.

Student Edition notes:
Badge: Prototype Lab. Show V1, Test, Upgrade, Show.

## Lesson 7: Fair Split Mission

Category: Values, Ethics & Civic Duty.

Subcategories:
- Moral Reasoning.
- Golden And Platinum Rule.
- Decision Architecture.
- Service And Contribution.

Primary methods:
- Concrete-representational-abstract.
- Social narrative.
- Play-based learning.
- Explicit teaching.

Student Edition mission:
Help split the treasure fairly.

Duration:
- Standard: 10-15 minutes.
- Low-demand: split 4 items between 2 people.

Materials:
- Small snack pieces, blocks, stickers, or pretend treasure.
- Two plates or bowls.
- Puppet or family member.

Objective:
Ezra will split items fairly and discuss whether fair always means the same.

Why it matters:
Fairness is easiest to learn with concrete objects before abstract morality.

Setup:
Use 6-10 identical items. Add a puppet who likes or needs something different.

Steps:
1. Say, "We are making it fair."
2. Split 4 items equally: one for you, one for me.
3. Ask if it is fair.
4. Add twist: Puppet says, "I only want one because I am not hungry" or "I need the bigger piece because I am bigger."
5. Talk about same vs what someone needs.
6. End with a helper action: give someone their share kindly.

Questions:
- Did everyone get enough?
- Is fair always the same?
- What did the other person want?
- What is one kind way to give it?

Metric:
- Splits objects one-to-one.
- Identifies same/equal.
- Notices another preference or need.

Evidence:
- Teacher note on fairness reasoning.

Supports:
- Visual: two plates.
- Sensory: use non-food if snack distracts.
- Communication: point to fair/not fair.
- Transition: use before snack.

Make easier:
- Use 4 objects and equal split only.

Make harder:
- Use odd number.
- Add preference/need.
- Ask him to invent a fair solution.

Recovery:
If food sharing creates distress, use blocks or stickers.

Student Edition notes:
Show treasure and two bowls. Keep moral wording simple: "Help everyone get what they need."
